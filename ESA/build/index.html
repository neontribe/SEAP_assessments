A Quick Guide To ESA

Please choose from the options below to get familiar with ESA and for tips on taking your assessment. Not in the mood for reading? Practice some assessment questions instead →

ESA is an allowance for people who can’t work because of illness or disability.

To get ESA you have to pass through the Work Capability Assessment.

The first part of the assessment involves filling in a medical questionnaire form called the ESA 50. Most people then go on to have a face-to-face assessment.

When filling out the form

  • Don’t be intimidated by the questions, or by the form’s length. The questions are simple and are not trying to catch you out
  • Give enough detail about how your condition affects you
  • Get help if you need it from a Citizen’s Advice Bureau or advice centre

A medical professional carries out the assessment, but the final decision on your claim is made by a decision maker at the Department of Work and Pensions (DWP for short) based on recommendations by the assessor.

When making their recommendation, the assessor will consider:

  • The answers you give on the medical questionnaire.
  • All the evidence that you send with the questionnaire or provide at the face-to-face assessment.
  • The answers you give at your face-to-face assessment.

Just a couple of things to remember:

  • Make a list of your health conditions and disabilities, so that you don’t forget to mention them at the assessment. Try taking the test on our website first, where you can save and print off your most important answers!
  • Plan your journey to the assessment centre in advance. Don’t be late!

The assessor looks at different activities that you might be asked to carry out if you were in a job or undertaking a work related activity. These are divided into physical and mental activities.

Here are some tips:

  • Don’t put on a brave face when talking about your disability or health condition; be clear about how much it affects you.
  • Your condition may change from day to day, be clear about how it impacts on you on your worst day.
  • Equally, don’t pretend things are worse than they really are. Knowingly giving false answers as part of a benefit application is benefit fraud, which is a criminal offence.

You will be given a score from 0 to 15 for each of these physical and mental activity questions.

For example, for the physical activity of 'using your hands', if you can’t press a button like a telephone keypad or turn the pages of a book, you will score 15 points. If you have full use of your hands you will score 0 points. If you can’t write with a pen or pencil, or have problems using a keyboard, your score will be in between.

  • You will be able to claim back reasonable expenses for getting to your assessment, so keep any bus or train tickets
  • You have the right to be accompanied at your assessment. You can ask a relative, friend, carer, or an advocate or support worker to go with you
  • You have a right to complain about the assessment if you don’t think it was conducted fairly

You will receive a letter from the DWP telling you the outcome of your application. The letter will explain how the decision was reached, including the points you scored in your test, and the amount you will be paid.

If you are unhappy with the decision, you can request a mandatory reconsideration. If you are still unhappy, you can appeal against it. We recommend that you get some support with this from a local Citizen’s Advice Bureau or advice centre.

To start your application for ESA, do one of the following:

  • Phone 0800 055 6688
  • Textphone 0800 023 4888
  • Go to the website www.gov.uk and print out the ESA1 form (PDF) opens new tab, complete it and send it to the address on the form, or take it to your local job centre.
